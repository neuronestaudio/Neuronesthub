Research Insight

Singing modulates mood, stress, cortisol, cytokine and neuropeptide activity in cancer patients and carers

Fancourt D, Williamon A, Carvalho LA, Steptoe A, Dow R, Lewis I

Deep Investigation

Context

By 2016, several studies had shown that singing reduces cortisol and improves mood. But the immune system side was almost entirely unexplored. Fancourt's team — spanning the Royal College of Music, Imperial College London, and UCL's Psychobiology Group — partnered with Tenovus Cancer Care in Wales to ask a much bigger question: does group singing actually modulate immune function at a biomarker level in people affected by cancer?

This was ambitious. They measured not just cortisol and mood, but beta-endorphin, oxytocin, AND ten individual cytokines — an extraordinarily broad biological panel for a singing study. The sample size (N=193) across five community choirs dwarfs most singing physiology research.

The headline findings were striking: cortisol dropped, the cytokine immune network activated, and those with the worst baseline mental health showed the greatest improvements. But some findings were also counterintuitive — oxytocin and beta-endorphin both decreased, contradicting the simple "singing = bonding hormone" narrative. This complexity makes the paper particularly valuable for NeuroNest Research Hub's balanced science communication approach.

For the NeuroNest content pipeline, this paper shifts the conversation from "sound makes you feel calm" to "self-generated sound may modulate your immune system." The immune activation angle is completely new territory for the brand and differentiates hard from relaxation-focused competitors.

Methodology deep-dive

  • Design: Multi-centre, single-arm preliminary study (no control group — this is the major limitation)
  • Setting: Five community choirs across South Wales, all run by Tenovus Cancer Care
  • Subjects: 193 total participants in three groups: carers (n=72), bereaved carers (n=66), cancer patients (n=55). Excluded: pregnant, currently receiving chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or oral immunosuppressive drugs. Regular choir participants — self-selecting sample.
  • Intervention: One hour of group singing (a regular choir rehearsal session). Not a standardized intervention — natural choir session.
  • Timing: Pre and post measurements taken immediately before and after the singing session.
  • Saliva collection: Passive drool method — subjects drooled into tubes. Samples immediately frozen and stored at -80°C until analysis.
  • Biomarker panel:
  • Psychological measures: Visual analogue mood scales, perceived stress scales
  • Statistics: Paired t-tests for pre-post comparisons. Pearson correlations between biomarker changes and psychological scores. Adjusted significance threshold for cytokines: p<0.002 (Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons). Subgroup analyses by participant group.
  • Blinding: Not possible — single-arm design.

Sound protocol specifics

  • This is NOT a controlled sound intervention study: The "intervention" was a regular community choir session. No standardization of repertoire, tempo, pitch, volume, or breathing patterns.
  • Duration: 1 hour total singing time
  • Group size: Five choirs across South Wales — group sizes not specified per choir
  • Repertoire: Not specified — presumably varied across the five choirs
  • No audio measurements: No dB levels, frequency analysis, or acoustic characterization
  • From Dion's sound engineering perspective: The lack of acoustic characterization is the biggest limitation for NeuroNest's content. We don't know if the effects depend on specific vocal frequencies, volume levels, breathing patterns driven by the music's structure, or whether any group singing of any type would produce the same results. This paper tells us THAT singing does something; it doesn't tell us WHAT about the singing does it. The Vickhoff paper addresses this gap for HRV; nobody has yet done it for immune markers.

Key findings (beyond the headline)

  • Mood: Significant reductions in negative affect and increases in positive affect across ALL participant groups (P<0.01). Consistent across all five choirs.
  • Cortisol: Decreased significantly after singing across all groups
  • Cytokine network activation: Significant increases in GM-CSF, IL17, IL2, IL4, TNFα, sIL-2rα, sTNFr1 after singing — suggestive of a general activation of the cytokine network
  • IFNγ and IL6: Did NOT reach adjusted significance (p<0.002), though trends were present
  • Cortisol-cytokine inverse correlation: Cortisol reductions correlated negatively with cytokine increases (IL2: r=-0.485; TNFα: r=-0.326; sIL2rα: r=-0.339; IL4: r=-0.458). Greater stress reduction → greater immune activation.
  • Beta-endorphin DECREASED (counterintuitive — endorphins expected to increase with social/pleasurable activity). Authors suggest this may reflect a general reduction in stress/arousal rather than a specific opioid effect.
  • Oxytocin DECREASED (contradicts studies showing singing increases oxytocin). Authors propose that reduced stress/arousal during singing may have inhibited peripheral OXT secretion — consistent with the Schladt et al. 2017 finding that choir singing in an elite/performance context also decreased OXT.
  • Floor effect: Those with lowest baseline mental wellbeing showed the greatest improvements — strongest effects in people who needed it most.
  • Bereaved carers: Showed the largest cortisol reductions of any group.

What the authors didn't say

  • No control group: This is the paper's fatal flaw for causal inference. Without a comparison condition (e.g., sitting quietly, chatting, listening to music, group activity without singing), the observed effects cannot be attributed specifically to singing. They could be caused by: social gathering, physical activity of singing, distraction from worry, warm environment, or simply the passage of 1 hour (diurnal cortisol decline).
  • Diurnal cortisol confound: Cortisol naturally declines across the day. Without a no-singing control at the same time of day, the observed cortisol reduction could partially or wholly reflect normal diurnal cycling. The authors acknowledge this but cannot control for it.
  • Self-selecting sample: These are people who CHOSE to join cancer choirs. They are likely predisposed to benefit from singing, more socially connected, and potentially different in personality from non-joiners. This limits generalizability.
  • Single time point: One hour of singing, measured once. No data on whether effects persist beyond the immediate post-singing window, whether they cumulate with repeated sessions, or whether they have any meaningful impact on cancer outcomes.
  • Mixed cancer types and stages: The patient group included various cancer diagnoses at varying treatment stages. This heterogeneity prevents any cancer-specific conclusions.
  • The cytokine activation pattern is complex to interpret: Increased TNFα and IL17 are typically considered pro-inflammatory. Is immune "activation" good or bad? The authors frame it as positive (reversing stress-mediated immunosuppression), but a pro-inflammatory cytokine increase could theoretically be harmful in some cancer contexts. This nuance is often lost in popular reporting.
  • Oxytocin measurement via saliva is debated: The relationship between salivary and central oxytocin is not well established, and salivary OXT assays have known reliability issues.
  • No acoustic data means we can't build NeuroNest content around "what to sing" — only around "that singing does something."

Cross-references in NeuroNest Research Hub

  • Immune comparison: Kreutz et al. 2004 — showed singing (vs listening) increased S-IgA (different immune marker) in amateur choir
  • Oxytocin context: Schladt et al. 2017 — also found OXT decrease during choir singing (elite choir). Good & Russo 2022 — found OXT increase during community choir singing. The discrepancy likely reflects stress/arousal context.
  • Endocrine mechanism: Keeler et al. 2015 — measured ACTH and oxytocin during singing; found ACTH decrease (stress axis dampening)
  • Physiological mechanism: Vickhoff et al. 2013 — explains HOW singing affects autonomic function (via respiratory entrainment at 0.1 Hz) which may be the upstream driver of the endocrine/immune changes Fancourt observes
  • NeuroNest narrative position: This is the "immune endpoint" paper. Bernardi → Vickhoff → Fancourt tells a story from respiratory mechanics → autonomic synchronization → downstream immune modulation.
